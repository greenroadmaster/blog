# Buddy System: The Foundation of Safe Diving

*Instructor's Perspective | June 2026*

---

## The Story

I lost my buddy at 18 meters. One second he was beside me. The next—gone.

My heart stopped. Every training protocol came back: Look around. Ascend to 5 meters. Wait 60 seconds. Look again.

I was alone, in the water, at depth, watching the seconds tick by.

Ninety seconds later, he emerged from behind coral—had seen a turtle and gotten distracted. We surfaced safely.

But those 90 seconds felt like an hour. **That's when I truly understood: your buddy is your lifeline. Not your entertainment partner. Not your swimming friend. Your survival system.**

---

## The Buddy System Philosophy

Buddy diving isn't optional. It's **mandatory for survival.**

The buddy system works on one principle: **Two brains are better than one. Two sets of eyes see more. Two people can help each other in ways one person cannot.**

But it only works if BOTH people understand their responsibility.

---

## Pre-Dive Buddy Checks: The "Buddy Check" Protocol

Before you even get in the water, you and your buddy must verify each other's equipment.

**The ABCDEF Check:**

**A - Air** (Tanks pressurized?)
- Both tanks at 200+ bar
- Valve working properly
- SPG (pressure gauge) visible and readable

**B - Buoyancy** (BCD functional?)
- Inflate from oral inflator—does it hold air?
- Inflate from power inflation—does button work?
- Can you deflate? (Critical—you must be able to vent air)
- Weights secure and accessible

**C - Clarity** (Mask & vision?)
- Mask clears when you blow through nose
- Lens isn't foggy or cracked
- You can see your buddy clearly

**D - Ditch** (Can you ditch weights?)
- Find your weight belt
- You can unbuckle it in 3 seconds
- Weights won't get caught on anything
- Your buddy can verify this

**E - Everything Secure?**
- No dangling hoses
- Gauges clipped to harness
- Fins won't fall off
- Nothing will catch on coral

**F - Final Agreement**
- What's the plan? (Depth, duration, direction)
- What signals will we use?
- What's the turnaround time?
- Where's the exit?

Both people must say "Okay" to all six. If either person is uncertain, **stop. Fix it. Try again.**

I've had divers refuse to buddy check properly and then act surprised when things go wrong. There's no surprise. You knew equipment wasn't verified, and you dived anyway.

## Underwater Buddy Rules: The Non-Negotiables

### Rule 1: Stay Together

**You should be able to reach your buddy within one arm's reach at all times.**

Not "generally nearby." Not "we can see each other." **Within arm's reach.**

In low visibility (less than 5 meters), this means actually holding hands or touching equipment.

Why? Because:
- If something goes wrong, you can help immediately
- You can communicate through touch
- You won't get separated if surge or current pushes you
- You're responsible for each other's safety

### Rule 2: Regular Check-Ins

Every 30-60 seconds, make eye contact with your buddy and confirm: **"Okay?"**

They respond: "Okay."

This simple exchange tells you:
- They're conscious
- They're breathing normally
- They're not panicking
- They can see you and acknowledge you

If they don't respond, or if their response seems hesitant, **end the dive.** Ascend slowly, surface, talk about it.

### Rule 3: Air Management is Shared Responsibility

You're not just watching your own air. You're watching your buddy's consumption too.

**Watch for signs of rising air consumption:**
- Faster breathing (visible bubble stream)
- Checking pressure gauge more frequently
- Wider eyes
- Faster swimming

All of these are signs your buddy is anxious or stressed. If you see this pattern:
- Reduce depth
- Slow the pace
- Move to shallower water
- If it doesn't improve: **End the dive**

### Rule 4: Buddy Integrity is Sacred

This might sound dramatic. It's not.

Your buddy trusts you with their life. You trust them with yours.

This means:
- You don't leave them for better views
- You don't ditch them because you want to go deeper
- You don't surface separately
- You don't take unnecessary risks because you're showing off

A true diving partner is more valuable than any fish, any coral, any photograph.

## What a True Buddy Does

A good buddy isn't just someone who dives near you. A true buddy:

**Before the dive:**
- Verifies your equipment thoroughly
- Asks questions about your experience level
- Listens to your concerns
- Plans the dive together (not tells you the plan)

**During the dive:**
- Stays within arm's reach
- Checks on you regularly
- Notices if you're struggling
- Turns the dive if you're tired or anxious
- Celebrates good moments with you (pointing out fish, sharing excitement)

**After the dive:**
- Debriefs with you (what went well? what was hard?)
- Doesn't criticize or make you feel small
- Helps you improve for next time
- Books the next dive together

**In emergencies:**
- Stays calm when you're panicking
- Shares air without hesitation
- Ascends with you safely
- Stays with you until you reach the surface and assistance

## When Buddy Diving Goes Wrong

### Scenario 1: Your Buddy Separates

**At 20 meters, you look and your buddy is gone.**

What you do:
1. Look around for 30-60 seconds (stay calm)
2. If you find them: make eye contact, confirm both are okay
3. If you DON'T find them: ascend to 5 meters
4. Wait 60 seconds at 5 meters, looking around
5. If still no buddy: make a safe ascent to surface
6. Signal boat or shore for help
7. **Do NOT dive deeper looking for them**

### Scenario 2: Your Buddy Panics

**At 15 meters, your buddy's eyes go wide and they start breathing fast.**

What you do:
1. Make eye contact (don't grab them—that can increase panic)
2. Hold up your hand in a calm gesture
3. Signal: "Slow down, breathe slowly"
4. Ascend slowly together
5. Let them hold your hand if needed
6. Surface at controlled rate
7. On boat: debrief, don't criticize

### Scenario 3: You're Panicking

**You start feeling anxious or afraid. Your breathing gets fast.**

What you do:
1. Stop swimming
2. Make eye contact with buddy
3. Signal: "Not okay"
4. Let them guide you to shallower depth
5. Breathe slowly and deeply
6. Once calm: decide if you continue at shallower depth or surface
7. No shame. This is the right choice.

## Why Buddy Diving Matters (Beyond Safety)

Yes, buddy diving saves lives. That's the obvious reason.

But there's another reason: **shared experience.**

Every memorable dive I have, I have with a buddy. The turtle we saw together. The massive school of jacks. The time we got caught in a current and had to swim hard to exit.

Those moments, experienced with another person who understands what you're feeling, are irreplaceable.

That's why diving partners often become lifelong friends. You've literally trusted each other with your lives.

---

## What I Tell Every New Buddy Team

Before the dive:
"You're responsible for each other. Not just for equipment checks. For well-being. For calm. For safety. If one of you is tired, you both surface. If one is scared, you both slow down. You're not a pair of divers—you're a team. Act like it."

After the dive:
"How did that feel? What would you do differently? Did you trust each other? Good. That's everything."
