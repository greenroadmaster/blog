# Safety Protocols & Emergency Management Underwater

*Instructor's Perspective | June 2026*

---

## The Story

A diver panicked at 15 meters. Eyes wide. Breathing fast. Starting to ascend rapidly.

His buddy didn't grab him (that increases panic). Didn't force him down.

Made eye contact. Grabbed his hand gently. Established calm presence. Signaled slow ascent.

Diver calmed. They ascended together at safe rate. Made safety stop. Surfaced safely. No DCS. No injury.

**Presence beats force every time.**

---

## The Emergency Management Philosophy

Underwater emergencies happen. They're rare, but they happen.

The difference between "emergency handled" and "tragedy" is **training + calm + procedure.**

Not heroics. Not rushing. Just: **Know what to do. Do it calmly. Communicate.**

## Core Emergency Protocols

### Emergency 1: Out of Air

**If YOUR air runs low (500 bar):**

1. **Don't panic** (panic makes you breathe faster, wastes remaining air)
2. **Find your buddy** (signal: point to pressure gauge, then point to buddy's regulator)
3. **Signal clearly:** Thumbs up + point to their regulator = "I need air"
4. **Establish contact:** Buddy offers their alternate air source (SPG regulator or backup second stage)
5. **Breathe together:** Share air with buddy while ascending
6. **Ascend slowly** (max 10m/min) while holding hands
7. **Both monitor air** (shared air is being used twice as fast)
8. **Safety stop at 5m if possible** (if air permits)
9. **Surface together**

**If BUDDY is out of air (you notice):**

1. Watch for signs: rapid descent, wide eyes, desperate movements
2. Move close and make eye contact
3. Offer your alternate air source (don't wait for them to ask—they may panic)
4. Guide them upward gently (hand on arm)
5. Both ascend together at controlled rate
6. Monitor their breathing (make sure they're actually breathing from alternate)
7. Repeat: calm, slow, together

**Key Point:** Out of air is manageable. People die from out-of-air emergencies when they panic or try to ascend too fast.

### Emergency 2: Nitrogen Narcosis (Depth Sickness)

**When:** Usually 30m+, worse at 40m+

**What it feels like:** Drunk feeling, confusion, numbness, inappropriate behavior

**Why it happens:** Nitrogen gas becomes narcotic at high pressure

**Real example:** A diver at 40m decides to explore 60m. At 55m, he's so narced that he thinks it's a good idea to remove his regulator. His buddy saves him.

**How to recognize it:**
- Wide eyes
- Slow, deliberate movements
- Staring into space
- Inappropriate grins or laughter
- Not responding to signals

**What to do:**
1. Ascend to shallower depth (usually 15m or less)
2. Symptoms disappear almost immediately
3. If severe: abort dive, surface together

**How to prevent:**
- Don't go too deep as beginner
- Stay within your training limits
- Monitor your buddy for signs
- Know your own susceptibility (varies person to person)

### Emergency 3: Shallow Water Blackout (Breath-Hold Diving)

**What it is:** Losing consciousness while holding your breath

**Why it happens:** Hyperventilation before breath-hold

**The scenario:** Person hyperventilates (fast, deep breathing). Lowers their CO2 levels. Holds their breath. Swims around. Oxygen drops below critical threshold. **No urge to breathe** (because CO2 is low). Loses consciousness underwater.

**Prevention (CRITICAL):**
- **NEVER hyperventilate before breath-hold**
- Breathe normally
- Hold your breath when ready
- Surface when you feel air hunger
- Never swim far from buddy while breath-holding

**What to do if someone blacks out:**
- Grab them
- Bring to surface immediately
- Support their airway
- Give rescue breathing if needed
- Get medical help fast

### Emergency 4: Panic

**Signs of panic:**
- Wide eyes, rapid breathing
- Erratic movements
- Gasping or gulping (trying to take in more air than regulator provides)
- Abandoning plan (swimming randomly upward)

**What NOT to do:**
- **Don't grab them hard** (triggers more panic)
- Don't yell (they can't hear well)
- Don't force them down

**What TO do:**
1. Make eye contact (show calm face)
2. Breathe slowly yourself (model calm breathing)
3. Give them space (don't crowd them)
4. Use hand signals: "Okay?" with questioning gesture
5. If they signal "not okay": help them to shallower water
6. Let them hold your hand if needed
7. Ascend slowly together
8. On surface: debrief, comfort, no criticism

**Why this works:** Panic is contagious. But so is calm. Your presence matters.

---

### Emergency 5: Equipment Failure

**Regulator fails (hard to breathe):**
- Switch to backup second stage
- If no backup: signal buddy, share their regulator
- Ascend slowly
- Get equipment serviced before next dive

**BCD won't inflate:**
- Ditch weight belt (establish positive buoyancy)
- Use buddy's BCD (they inflate it, you cling to them)
- Ascend slowly together
- Get BCD repaired

**Mask floods (water in mask):**
- Blow air out of nose to clear it (equalizes pressure)
- Water exits, air replaces it
- Vision returns
- Continue diving
- If you can't clear it: remove mask, surface slowly, replace at surface

**Fin breaks:**
- Remove both fins (using broken fins is exhausting)
- Swim using body only (undulating motion)
- OR: ask buddy to tow you (hold hands, they propel you both)
- Exit slowly
- Get fins repaired

## Emergency Protocols Summary

| Emergency | Immediate Action | Secondary | Exit |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|------|
| **Out of Air** | Find buddy, offer air | Ascend together | Safety stop, surface |
| **Nitrogen Narcosis** | Ascend to shallower depth | Breathe normally | Continue or abort |
| **Panic** | Calm presence, eye contact | Hand-hold, descend or ascend slowly | Surface with buddy |
| **Equipment Fail** | Switch to backup or buddy's | Ditch weight if needed | Slow ascent together |

## The Golden Rules

**Rule 1: Slow**
Fast decisions underwater are usually bad decisions. Slow, deliberate actions work.

**Rule 2: Calm**
Your buddy watches your face. If you're panicking, they panic. If you're calm, they calm.

**Rule 3: Communicate**
Hand signals, eye contact, touch. Make sure your buddy knows what you're doing.

**Rule 4: Buddy**
You are not alone. Your buddy is your survival system. Trust them.

**Rule 5: Surface Safely**
The ascent is when most accidents happen. Slow, controlled, together.

## What I Drill Every Student

Before certification:

"We practice emergency procedures because emergencies ARE your training. When something goes wrong, your body will do what you've practiced.

You haven't really practiced until you've done it underwater, breathing through a regulator, at some depth, stressed.

That's why we do these drills. So when it matters, you don't think. You just do."

After certification:

"You're certified now. That means you know basic procedures. But you will forget them under stress unless you keep practicing.

Refresh every year. Drill with your buddy. Take advanced courses.

The best diver isn't the one with the most certifications. It's the one who stays sharp."

## The Truth About Emergencies

Most underwater emergencies are prevented by:
- Good training
- Good equipment maintenance
- Good pre-dive planning
- Good buddy discipline

The emergency that kills you is the one you didn't see coming because you skipped one of those four things.

The emergency you survive is the one you prepared for.
