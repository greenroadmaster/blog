# Managing the Dive: Real-Time Decision-Making

*Instructor's Perspective | June 2026*

---

## The Story

At 22 meters, I noticed my buddy's breathing pattern change. Faster. More frequent bubble bursts.

He nodded "okay" when I checked. But his eyes said otherwise.

I signaled: "Let's go up."

He hesitated. Clearly wanted to stay. But I ended the dive.

On the boat, he admitted: "I was getting nervous at depth. I wanted to stay because you seemed comfortable, but inside I was getting scared."

**That was the right call.** Pushing through would have meant panic.

---

## The Real-Time Decision Framework

During every dive, you're constantly assessing: **Is this safe to continue?**

### The Safety Checklist (Every 30 Seconds)

**Air Consumption:**
- Is my breathing normal? (If fast, I'm stressed)
- Can I see my buddy's bubble stream? (Should be steady)
- Do I have enough air? (Half my starting pressure = turnaround point)

**Depth & Navigation:**
- Am I at expected depth? (Not drifting deeper)
- Do I know where I am? (Can find exit)
- Can I exit safely right now? (This is key)

**Body & Mind:**
- Am I cold? (Shivering = time to ascend)
- Am I tired? (Heavy breathing = tired)
- Am I anxious? (This is a signal)
- Am I bored or impatient? (Sign that dive isn't right)

**Buddy:**
- Can I see my buddy? (Always)
- Do they look okay? (Check their face)
- Are they breathing normally? (Steady or fast?)

**Environment:**
- Is visibility as expected? (Or degrading?)
- Is current as expected? (Stronger than planned?)
- Is anything unusual?

### If Anything Concerns You: End the Dive

Not "maybe end." Not "probably end." **End it.**

There is no medal for pushing through discomfort. There is a hyperbaric chamber for people who panic.

---

## Real-Time Adjustments You Can Make

### Adjustment 1: Reduce Depth

"We planned 20m, but visibility is poor at this depth. Let's stay at 12m instead."

Shallower = more light = easier = less stress = better experience.

### Adjustment 2: Shorten Duration

"We planned 45 minutes, but current is stronger than expected. Let's do 30 minutes instead."

This protects air consumption and prevents fatigue.

### Adjustment 3: Change Direction

"We were going left, but current is pushing us right. Let's go right instead."

Work with nature, not against it.

### Adjustment 4: Move Closer to Reef/Exit

"Visibility just dropped. Let's stay close to the reef instead of exploring open water."

This prevents disorientation.

### Adjustment 5: Slow the Pace

"Everyone's breathing harder than expected. Let's relax, hover for 2 minutes, then continue slowly."

A slow dive is better than a fast, stressed dive.

---

## When To End a Dive Immediately

**Non-negotiable reasons to surface:**

1. **Air runs too low** (500 bar = start ascending)
2. **Visibility disappears** (can't see buddy or landmarks)
3. **Buddy signals distress** (they want to go up)
4. **You're panicking** (even slightly)
5. **Sudden illness** (nausea, dizziness, chest pain)
6. **Equipment failure** (regulator breathing hard, BCD won't inflate)
7. **Major current change** (can't swim against it)

---

## What I Tell Every Diver

"Your job isn't to complete the dive. Your job is to notice when something isn't right, and tell me.

The dive that goes wrong is the one where someone feels uncomfortable but doesn't say anything.

I'd rather surface early and have everyone safe than keep diving and have someone panic."
