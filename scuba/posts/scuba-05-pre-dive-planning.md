# Pre-Dive Planning: From Booking to Briefing

*Instructor's Perspective | June 2026*

---

## The Story

A group of four booked a dive at a site I'd never verified. The shop assured them: "Perfect for beginners."

We showed up at the site. 

Visibility: 5 meters. Current: 2 knots pushing northeast. Bottom: sandy at 12m, then sudden drop-off to a wall at 25m.

For beginners? This was a nightmare waiting to happen.

One diver got separated trying to follow the wall. Another was breathing so hard from fighting current that he ran low on air at 20 minutes. The third got claustrophobic in the poor visibility.

We aborted the dive. Nobody got hurt, but nobody had fun either.

That day, I made a rule: **I research every site personally. I talk to operators who dive there regularly. I don't trust reviews written by people I don't know in conditions I haven't verified.**

---

## Pre-Dive Planning: The Foundation

Pre-dive planning isn't bureaucracy. It's the difference between a great dive and a disaster.

A proper dive plan answers these questions:

**Where?** (Site selection)  
**When?** (Season & conditions)  
**How deep?** (Depth limits)  
**How long?** (Dive duration)  
**What conditions?** (Current, visibility, hazards)  
**Who else is there?** (Skill levels of group)  
**What can go wrong?** (Emergency planning)  

If you can't answer all of these, **you're not ready to dive yet.**

## Phase 1: Site Research

### Choose Your Site Wisely

**Questions to ask:**
- What's the typical depth?
- What's the current pattern?
- What's visibility like this time of year?
- Are there hazards? (Walls, caves, sharp coral, strong surge)
- What skill level does the site recommend?
- Is it known for anything special? (Turtles? Sharks? Macro?)

**Where to get information:**
- Talk to local dive operators (they know current conditions)
- Join diving forums (real divers share honest experiences)
- Check dive shop reviews on TripAdvisor (filter by recent)
- Ask your instructor or experienced divers

**Red flags:**
- "Everyone says it's easy" (everyone isn't your guide)
- "You'll see tons of sharks" (sharks are fine, but it suggests challenging conditions)
- "Current can be strong sometimes" (ask how strong, how often)
- Shop won't answer your questions (find another shop)

### Verify Current Conditions

The best site in the world is dangerous if conditions are wrong on the day you dive.

Call the operator and ask:
- "What were conditions like on dives yesterday?"
- "What's the current forecast?"
- "What's visibility right now?"
- "Has anyone had trouble recently?"

A good operator will tell you honestly. A bad operator will tell you what sells.

## Phase 2: Personal Preparation

Your mind and body need to be ready. A distracted diver is a dangerous diver.

### The Night Before

**Sleep:**
- Get 8+ hours of quality sleep
- No alcohol the night before
- Go to bed at normal time (don't oversleep)

**Mental prep:**
- Visualize the dive going smoothly
- Review the site plan mentally
- Think through emergency procedures

### The Morning Of

**Breakfast:**
- Eat something substantial but not heavy
- No heavy meats or greasy foods
- Toast + jam, yogurt + granola, rice + vegetables = good
- Empty stomach = low energy
- Heavy stomach = nausea at depth

**Hydration:**
- Drink water, not coffee or soft drinks
- Coffee is a diuretic (dehydrates you)
- Hydrated divers have slower nitrogen absorption (less DCS risk)

**Avoid:**
- Alcohol (impairs judgment, dehydrates)
- Too much caffeine (anxiety at depth)
- Heavy physical activity (tire yourself out)
- Stress (anxiety underwater is dangerous)

### Gear Inspection

Before leaving home:
- Check all seals on regulators
- Test BCD inflation/deflation
- Check all hoses for cracks
- Verify backup computer battery
- Check fins fit properly
- Inspect wetsuit for rips

If anything is questionable: don't take it. Bring backup.

## Phase 3: On-Site Briefing

The briefing is where the plan becomes real.

### What a Good Briefing Includes

**Site Layout:**
- Where's the entry point?
- What's the immediate area like? (Shallow sand? Rocks? Coral?)
- What's the planned route? (Follow left wall? Straight out? Zigzag?)
- What are major landmarks? (How will we navigate back?)

**Depth & Time:**
- What's our target depth?
- What's the maximum we'll go?
- How long will we stay down?
- When will we start ascending? (Usually when someone signals low air or time limit)

**Current & Conditions:**
- Which direction is the current flowing?
- How strong is it?
- How will it affect our dive? (Do we enter upcurrent and exit downcurrent?)
- What's visibility like today?

**Emergency Procedures:**
- What if someone's low on air? (Abort and ascend)
- What if someone gets separated? (Surface, look for boat, use whistle)
- What if a diver panics? (Slow ascent, buddy support)
- Where's the boat after we surface?

**Marine Life & Hazards:**
- Are there any dangerous fish? (Sea urchins, stonefish, etc.)
- Should we avoid touching anything?
- What will we probably see?
- What's off-limits? (No chasing fish, no touching coral)

**Buddy Integrity:**
- Who's your buddy? (Name and face)
- Will you stay together?
- How will you communicate? (Hand signals review)
- What's the turnaround signal? (Thumbs up = end of dive)

### The Pre-Dive Talk I Give Every Group

"Here's our plan. Here's the site. Here's the depth. Here's how long we're going.

If at any point you feel:
- Tired
- Cold
- Anxious
- Uncomfortable
- Unsure

Signal me. We turn back. No questions. No judgment. Safety first.

This isn't a race. We're here to enjoy the ocean, not prove anything.

Buddy stays with buddy. If you get separated, surface safely and look for the boat.

Questions?"

## Phase 4: Final Checks at Water's Edge

### Equipment Check

- All connections tight?
- Tanks pressurized?
- Regulators breathing smoothly?
- BCD holds air?
- Weights secure?
- Fins on properly?

### The Buddy Check (ABCDEF)

Already covered in the Buddy System post, but do it again:
- **A**ir pressure good?
- **B**uoyancy works?
- **C**larity (mask clear)?
- **D**itch weights accessible?
- **E**verything secure?
- **F**inal okay?

Both people say "Okay" to all six. No exceptions.

## When Plans Change (Real-World Scenarios)

### Scenario 1: Weather Changes

You planned a dive. Weather forecast was good. You wake up—rain, wind, waves.

What to do:
1. Call operator
2. Ask: "Is dive still safe today?"
3. If operator says yes: trust them (they know local conditions)
4. If operator cancels: accept it
5. Reschedule or find alternative site

**Never pressure an operator to dive in bad conditions.**

### Scenario 2: You're Sick

You woke up with a cold. Stuffy nose. You're supposed to dive.

What to do:
**Don't dive.**

Here's why:
- You can't equalize ear pressure with congestion
- You're susceptible to sinus barotrauma
- You're more likely to panic underwater
- You risk decompression sickness

Reschedule. Your health matters.

### Scenario 3: Unexpected Conditions

You planned a calm site. You arrive—visibility is 3 meters (half expected) and current is strong (not forecasted).

What to do:
1. Talk to operator
2. Ask: "Can beginners dive these conditions?"
3. Consider: "Is this too advanced for my group?"
4. If yes, it's too advanced: abort
5. If maybe: start shallow and see how everyone feels

## The Planning Mindset

Good planning isn't about control. It's about respect.

You're preparing to enter an environment more powerful than you. Planning is how you show that respect.

A diver who dives without a plan is saying: "I don't respect this enough to prepare."

A diver who plans carefully is saying: "I respect the ocean. I respect my buddy. I respect my own life."

---

## What I Tell Every Diver Before Their First Dive

"We have a plan. We know the site. We know the conditions. We know what to do if something goes wrong.

But plans change. Conditions surprise us. And that's okay.

Your job isn't to follow the plan perfectly. Your job is to notice when something isn't right, and tell me.

The dive that goes wrong is the one where someone feels uncomfortable but doesn't say anything.

You comfortable? Good. Let's dive."
