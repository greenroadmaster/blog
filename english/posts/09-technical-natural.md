# 09. Technical Knowledge + Natural Speech

*English Learning Journey | June 2026*

## The Story

I spent years in construction. I know technical terms: FIDIC conditions, variation orders, cost estimation, BOQ, IPC, CPC.

I know software: Primavera P6, RIB CostX, SAP.

That's a lot of jargon.

When I started learning English, I thought fluency meant using MORE technical terms.

I was wrong.

Fluency means using technical terms **naturally**.

Like a native speaker would.

## The Difference

**Technical but unnatural:**
"The implementation of the cost estimation methodology utilizing the Primavera P6 software, in accordance with FIDIC conditions, facilitated optimal resource allocation regarding the variation order protocol."

**Technical but natural:**
"We used Primavera P6 to track our costs and manage variations. When design changes came in, we could estimate the impact immediately instead of waiting weeks."

Same technical knowledge. One sounds like a robot. One sounds like a person.

## How Native Speakers Do It

They:
- Use simple sentences with technical terms inside
- Explain the **why** before the **what**
- Give examples
- Don't apologize for technical language

Example: "Data centres need massive electrical supply. We're talking 100+ megawatts for a large facility. That's the power consumption of a small city. So the challenge isn't just the building—it's making sure you have reliable power, backup systems in case of failure, and protection against power surges."

Notice: technical language embedded in clear, simple speech.

## The OPIc Connection

When I discussed data centres, I didn't use fancy language.

I explained technical concepts simply.

And that's what made me sound fluent.

## What This Taught Me

**Technical expertise without natural speech sounds pretentious.**

**Natural speech with technical knowledge sounds authoritative.**

Use the knowledge. But don't show it off.
